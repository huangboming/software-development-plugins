# Method Selection Guide

Choose a research method based on what the PM needs to learn. If the user already knows which method they want, skip selection and load the relevant prep reference.

## Decision Matrix

| Research question shape | Best method | Why |
|---|---|---|
| "Why do users do X?" / "What do users need?" | **Interview** | Open-ended exploration of motivations, mental models, unmet needs |
| "How many users experience X?" / "Which option do users prefer?" | **Survey** | Quantitative signal across a larger sample; validates hypotheses at scale |
| "Can users accomplish X with this design?" | **Usability test** | Direct observation of task success/failure; surfaces interaction problems |
| "Does this concept resonate before we build it?" | **Concept test** | Tests desirability and comprehension of a solution idea before investment |
| "What does the user's experience look like over time?" | **Diary study** | Captures behavior in context over days/weeks; reveals patterns invisible in a single session |
| "How do users think about grouping/labeling X?" | **Card sort** | Surfaces users' mental models for information architecture and navigation |

## Choosing Between Similar Methods

**Interview vs. survey:** Use interviews when you don't yet know the right questions to ask. Use surveys when you have specific hypotheses to validate quantitatively.

**Interview vs. concept test:** Use interviews for problem exploration (before a solution exists). Use concept tests to evaluate a specific solution idea.

**Usability test vs. concept test:** Usability tests require an interactive prototype or live product. Concept tests work with static descriptions, mockups, or landing pages.

**Diary study vs. interview:** Use diary studies when behavior unfolds over time and retrospective recall would distort the data. Use interviews when you need depth on a specific topic in a single session.

## Method Characteristics

| Method | Sample size | Timeline | PM effort | Output fidelity |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Interview | 5–8 participants | 1–2 weeks | High (conduct + debrief) | Rich qualitative |
| Survey | 30–200+ respondents | 1–2 weeks (design + collection) | Medium (design + analysis) | Quantitative + some qualitative |
| Usability test | 5–8 participants | 1–2 weeks | High (facilitate + observe) | Behavioral + qualitative |
| Concept test | 5–10 participants | 1–2 weeks | Medium (prepare stimulus + interview) | Qualitative reaction data |
| Diary study | 8–15 participants | 2–4 weeks | Low during study, high at analysis | Longitudinal qualitative |
| Card sort | 15–30 participants | 1–2 weeks | Low (setup + analysis) | Structural / categorical |

## Combining Methods

Common pairings:

- **Interviews → survey**: Interviews surface themes; survey quantifies which themes matter most.
- **Concept test → usability test**: Concept test validates the idea; usability test validates the execution.
- **Diary study → interviews**: Diary study captures patterns; follow-up interviews explore why.

When combining, produce a separate prep document for each method.
