# Concept Test Prep

Produces a prep document for testing a product or feature concept before building it. Works with static stimuli — descriptions, mockups, landing pages, prototypes — not interactive products (use usability test for those).

## Contents

1. [Prep Document Template](#prep-document-template) — Full template with stimulus design, discussion guide, and evaluation criteria
2. [Gotchas](#gotchas) — Problem-first anchoring, polite-positive bias, stimulus minimalism

## Discussion Guide

### Before Showing the Concept (10 min)

Establish the participant's current experience with the problem BEFORE exposing them to the concept:
- "Tell me about the last time you [relevant activity]."
- "What's the most frustrating part of [problem space]?"
- "How do you handle [specific pain point] today?"

### Concept Exposure (5 min)

Present the stimulus. Let the participant read/view it without commentary. Do not explain or sell.

### Reaction Questions (15–20 min)

**Comprehension:**
- "In your own words, what is this?"
- "Who do you think this is for?"

**Relevance:**
- "How does this relate to [the problem they described earlier]?"
- "Would this have helped in the situation you described?"

**Value:**
- "What's the most appealing part of this to you?"
- "What's missing or unclear?"
- "What concerns would you have about using this?"

**Behavior intent (use carefully — stated intent ≠ actual behavior):**
- "If this existed today, what would you do next?"
- "What would need to be true for you to try this?"

### Closing (5 min)
- "If you could change one thing about this concept, what would it be?"
- "Is there anything I should have asked about?"

## Evaluation Criteria

| Dimension | Signal to watch for |
|---|---|
| Comprehension | Can they describe it without prompting? Do they get it wrong? |
| Relevance | Do they connect it to their own experience unprompted? |
| Desirability | Emotional reaction — lean-in vs. polite nodding |
| Concerns | Dealbreakers vs. nice-to-haves |

## Gotchas

- **Establish the problem BEFORE showing the concept.** If participants see the solution first, they rationalize the problem to fit it. Problem-first anchoring surfaces genuine need vs. polite interest.
- **Do not explain the concept — let the stimulus speak.** If the concept requires verbal explanation to make sense, the stimulus is too weak. Revise it until it's self-explanatory. In a real launch, there's no moderator to clarify.
- **Watch for "polite positive" bias.** Participants default to being nice. "Yeah, that's interesting" is not a signal. Watch for behavioral cues: do they lean in? Ask follow-up questions unprompted? Connect it to their own life? Those are real signals.
- **Stated intent to use is unreliable.** "I'd definitely use this" means almost nothing. Focus on comprehension and relevance — those predict adoption better than self-reported intent.
- **Keep the stimulus minimal.** Overdetailed mockups anchor reactions to visual design instead of the concept. The stimulus should communicate what it is and why it matters, not how every pixel looks.
